# Edge Cases — assess-moat-sources

## Multi-Power compounds

Many durable franchises hold MULTIPLE Powers stacked (e.g., Apple = Branding + Switching Costs + Scale + Process Power). The output must enumerate each Power separately with its own benefit/barrier/durability — not collapse them into "Apple has a moat."

## Industries with no identifiable Power

Some industries are structurally unprotected (commodity manufacturing without scale, services without switching costs, fragmented professional services). The valid finding is: "No Power identifiable for any player in this industry; expect persistent ROIC near cost of capital + arbitrage from any pricing anomaly." This flows into a "do not invest for franchise economics" conclusion. Do not invent a Power to fill the slot.

## "Local" Network Economies

Local network effects (city-by-city marketplaces like rideshare) are weaker than global. The diagnostic should distinguish: is the network global (Facebook), continental (Uber-style city-by-city), or hyperlocal (single-venue effects)? Local networks are durable per locality but vulnerable to multi-homing and locality-by-locality competition.

## Regulatory exclusivity in transition

Patents about to expire, licences with finite term, regulatory exclusivity under political review — these are Cornered Resource for the remaining term only. Output must flag the expiry/review date and re-rate durability accordingly.

## Process Power vs flywheel

A "flywheel" (reinforcing loop in user activity → data → product → user) is typically Network Economies + Cornered Resource (data), NOT Process Power. Process Power is about internal operating routines, not external compounding loops. Watch for this conflation.

## Counter-Positioning that becomes Network/Scale

The standard pattern: a Counter-Positioning entrant (Netflix DVD vs Blockbuster) wins, reaches scale, and the durable Power BECOMES Scale or Network Economies (Netflix streaming → content scale). Counter-Positioning is the entry Power; the long-term franchise rests on a different Power. Output should articulate this transition for high-growth industries.

## Two-sided platform asymmetry

In two-sided platforms, switching costs and network economies often sit on one side only (e.g., merchants on Stripe have switching costs; consumers do not). Output must specify WHICH SIDE holds the Power, not assert it globally.

## Roll-up of Process Power holders

A roll-up acquiring a Process Power holder typically LOSES the Power post-close (senior-leader exits, culture dilution, integration disruption). Buildability assessment for roll-up of Process Power = NO by default, requires explicit case for why this acquisition will be different.

## Cross-Power erosion cascade

Erosion of one Power can cascade. Example: AI erodes Branding (info-asymmetry); reduced brand pull raises CAC; raised CAC erodes Scale Economies (advertising scale was a cost-share Power); now both Powers fade. Named-risks section should consider these cascades, not treat each Power's erosion in isolation.

## "Moat-light" archetypes

Some compounders hold no single strong Power but combine several medium ones (e.g., distributors with modest switching costs + density-based scale + local relationships). These are valid but the output must mark each Power as medium-intensity explicitly — not aggregate them into one undifferentiated "moat."
