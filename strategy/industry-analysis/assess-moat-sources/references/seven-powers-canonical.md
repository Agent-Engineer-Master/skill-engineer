# The Seven Powers — Canonical (Helmer 2016, verified 2026)

This is the verified canonical list per Hamilton Helmer's *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy* (Deep Strategy LLC, 2016), reconciled with 2024-26 commentary from Strategy Capital and the Acquired + Lenny's Newsletter interviews.

## Definitional core (do not paraphrase loosely)

A **Power** is a strategic position where the holder enjoys both:

- **Benefit:** a material improvement in cash flow via one or more of (a) higher realised price, (b) lower realised cost, (c) reduced investment requirement.
- **Barrier:** an obstacle that engenders in competitors (existing AND potential) an inability or unwillingness to engage in arbitrage that would erode the benefit.

**Both are required.** A benefit without a barrier is operational excellence — competitors will arbitrage. A barrier without a benefit is an obstacle to nothing.

This skill REJECTS any "Power" assertion missing either component.

## The 7 Powers — definitions, benefit, barrier

### 1. Scale Economies
- **Definition:** unit cost declines as production volume rises within an industry-specific cost structure.
- **Benefit:** cost (sub-scale entrants cannot match price at acceptable margin).
- **Barrier:** the sub-scale entrant's economic loss to reach MES exceeds the NPV of doing so. Forecasted-cost-of-entry trap.
- **Common location:** manufacturing, infrastructure, distribution, capital-intensive services.

### 2. Network Economies
- **Definition:** value to each user increases as more users join the network.
- **Benefit:** price (users pay more for higher-utility network) OR cost (lower acquisition cost via referrals).
- **Barrier:** late entrants offer lower utility per user; tipping dynamics → winner-take-most.
- **Common location:** marketplaces, social platforms, payment networks, communication tools, exchanges.

### 3. Counter-Positioning
- **Definition:** a new entrant adopts a business model the incumbent cannot copy without damaging its existing economics.
- **Benefit:** cost or price (entrant runs a structurally cheaper / better model).
- **Barrier:** incumbent self-cannibalisation — copying destroys the legacy P&L; incumbent rationally chooses inaction.
- **Common location:** disruptive entrants vs vertically-integrated incumbents (Vanguard vs active managers, Netflix DVD vs Blockbuster).

### 4. Switching Costs
- **Definition:** the customer's total cost of switching (financial + procedural + relational) exceeds the price differential a competitor can credibly offer.
- **Benefit:** price (incumbent extracts switching-cost-shielded margin) OR cost (lower retention spend).
- **Barrier:** competitor would need to compensate the customer's switching cost up-front — payback economics fail.
- **Common location:** ERP, vertical SaaS, EHRs, integrated workflow tools, banking.

### 5. Branding
- **Definition:** customers ascribe higher value to an objectively-identical offering because of associations (trust / safety / status / hedonic) built over years of consistent action.
- **Benefit:** price (price premium for objectively-equivalent product) OR cost (lower CAC via brand pull).
- **Barrier:** hysteresis — the brand took decades of consistent reinforcement; a new entrant cannot compress that timeline regardless of marketing spend.
- **Common location:** consumer-facing, luxury, trust-sensitive regulated categories, ingredients in safety-critical applications.

### 6. Cornered Resource
- **Definition:** preferential access on attractive terms to a coveted, hard-to-replicate asset — patent, licence, deposit, talent cluster, dataset, location.
- **Benefit:** any combination (lower input cost, exclusive output, lower investment).
- **Barrier:** the resource is legally / contractually / geographically corneblockchain or scarce by nature; alternative access does not exist on equivalent terms.
- **Common location:** R&D-driven (pharma patents), regulated exclusivity (radio spectrum, ESCO licences), resource-based (mining concessions), proprietary data assets.
- **Note:** regulatory exclusivity is a SUB-CASE of Cornered Resource, not a separate Power. "Regulatory barrier" naming is accepted but Helmer-canonical is "cornered resource (regulatory)".

### 7. Process Power
- **Definition:** organisational capability evolved over years through embedded routines, such that even a well-resourced rival cannot replicate it in a reasonable time.
- **Benefit:** cost or quality (rival cannot match unit economics or feature velocity).
- **Barrier:** hysteresis + complexity — the process is tacit, multi-causal, and embedded; senior-leader poaching does not transfer it (Toyota Production System).
- **Common location:** manufacturing excellence (Toyota), lithography (ASML), logistics (Amazon FBA), continuous-deployment platforms.

## NOT a Power (common confusions)

- **"First-mover advantage"** — a position, not a Power. First-mover is durable ONLY if it enabled a named Power (e.g., network economies via early liquidity).
- **"Operational excellence" / "execution"** — competitive, not structural. Becomes Process Power only with multi-year compounding AND evidence of failed replication.
- **"Differentiation"** — a symptom. The question is WHY the differentiation persists — which Power sustains it?
- **"Brand recognition"** — not Branding-as-Power unless coupled with measurable price premium AND multi-year build.
- **"Patent"** — Cornered Resource for patent term only. Durability tied to expiry date.
- **"Customer lock-in"** — Switching Costs only if quantified.
- **"Critical to the customer" / "adds value" / "high quality" / "expertise"** — descriptions, not mechanisms. REJECTED by validator.

## Power Progression (Helmer)

Powers become available at different industry/company stages:

| Stage | Powers typically establishable | Powers typically NOT yet establishable |
|-------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Origination** (pre-product-market fit, early shaping) | Counter-Positioning, Cornered Resource | Network Economies, Switching Costs, Branding, Process Power |
| **Takeoff** (scaling, share grab) | Scale Economies, Network Economies, Switching Costs, Branding | Process Power (still nascent) |
| **Stability** (mature industry) | Process Power; reinforcement of Takeoff Powers | Counter-Positioning (window typically closed) |

Cross-check the dominant Power against industry S-curve stage (per `analyze-trajectory`). A claim of "we will build Process Power in 2 years" in a 30-year mature industry is suspect.

## AI impact on the 7 Powers (Helmer commentary, 2024-26)

Per Helmer's published view (Lenny's interview + Strategy Capital posts), AI does NOT alter the framework but DOES asymmetrically affect specific Powers:

**Powers most exposed to erosion by AI (2026 horizon):**
- **Switching Costs:** AI agents can automate data extraction, schema translation, and workflow re-training. Hard-coded integration moats weaken when an LLM rewrites the integration layer in days.
- **Branding:** the information-asymmetry component erodes when AI shopping/research agents bypass brand-led discovery. The trust/safety component is more resilient.
- **Scale Economies:** AI lowers fixed-cost floors in content/software/customer-service businesses. Sub-scale entrants now reach MES faster.

**Powers most reinforced by AI:**
- **Cornered Resource (data):** proprietary training datasets become a stronger cornered resource — competitors cannot synthesise an equivalent.
- **Network Economies:** data network effects compound (more usage → better model → more usage).
- **Process Power:** AI codifies tacit process into models — incumbents who move first widen the gap.
