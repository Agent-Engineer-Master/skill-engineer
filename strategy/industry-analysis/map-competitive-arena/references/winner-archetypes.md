# Winner Archetypes

For each strategic group on the map, name the archetype that distinguishes group leaders from group followers. Five archetypes cover ~90% of observed leader patterns. Use "none emerging" only when justified.

## The 5 archetypes

### 1. Consolidator
**Wins via:** scale economics, M&A roll-up, cost leadership at volume, supplier leverage from buying power.
**Applies in:** mature/fragmented industries, regulated industries, distribution-heavy industries, industries where unit economics improve monotonically with scale.
**Signature moves:** programmatic M&A, regional roll-up, back-office consolidation, vertical integration of procurement.
**Examples (illustrative):** Waste Management in trash, Sysco in foodservice, US healthcare-system consolidators, Constellation Software in vertical SaaS.
**Failure mode:** consolidation hits diminishing returns; regulatory pushback; integration debt.

### 2. Innovator / specialist
**Wins via:** product or technology lead in a defensible niche, deep customer intimacy, premium pricing for differentiated capability.
**Applies in:** arena markets (high growth + high dispersion), early-stage industries, high-R&D industries (biotech, semiconductors, frontier software), niches inside fragmented industries.
**Signature moves:** R&D investment, narrow customer ICP, technical hiring, patent moats, sole-source supplier relationships.
**Examples (illustrative):** Linear in project management, ASML in EUV lithography, Veeva in life-sciences CRM.
**Failure mode:** niche commoditizes; large player copies feature; specialist over-narrows and TAM caps.

### 3. Platform orchestrator
**Wins via:** network effects, two-sided economics, ecosystem coordination, take-rate on third-party economic activity.
**Applies in:** marketplaces, app stores, exchanges, multi-sided industries where buyer and seller value scales with each other.
**Signature moves:** subsidized side recruitment, governance rule-setting, complementor cultivation, data flywheel monetization.
**Examples (illustrative):** Shopify in commerce, Stripe in payments, Visa/Mastercard in card networks, Uber in rideshare.
**Failure mode:** disintermediation (sides go direct), regulatory unbundling, multi-homing erodes lock-in.

### 4. Vertically-integrated player
**Wins via:** owning the critical layer competitors depend on, capturing margin from multiple value-chain steps, asymmetric information from end-to-end visibility.
**Applies in:** industries with thin midstream margins, regulated bottleneck industries, industries where component supply is constrained.
**Signature moves:** acquire upstream supplier, build proprietary distribution, in-source previously-outsourced layers.
**Examples (illustrative):** Tesla (cells + cars + service + charging), Apple (silicon + OS + retail), Costco (private label + warehouse + membership).
**Failure mode:** capital intensity exceeds returns; specialist competitors at each layer out-innovate the integrated player layer-by-layer.

### 5. Asset-light operator
**Wins via:** capital efficiency, franchise/partner leverage, network of independent operators under one brand, fast geographic scaling without owned-asset drag.
**Applies in:** scaling-stage industries, geographically dispersed services, hospitality, food, distribution, asset-rental industries.
**Signature moves:** franchise model, partner ecosystem, brand licensing, marketplace-of-operators design.
**Examples (illustrative):** McDonald's (franchising), Marriott (brand-managed hotels), Uber Eats (independent restaurants), Booking.com.
**Failure mode:** brand quality erodes through partner inconsistency; partners disintermediate; brand becomes commodity.

## "None emerging" — when this is the right answer

Sometimes a group has no clear winner archetype. Use this verdict when:

- **Pre-arena chaos** — market is too new; the winning model hasn't crystallized; multiple firms try multiple archetypes, none breaks out.
- **Deeply commoditized** — every firm in the group competes the same way; differentiation impossible; winners win by execution not strategy.
- **Genuine ambiguity** — equally-sized leaders pursue different archetypes successfully (signal that the group may bifurcate).

If declared, state WHY:
> "No clear winner archetype emerging. Group is in pre-arena chaos with 4 firms pursuing 4 different models (consolidator, innovator, platform, vertical). Watch for bifurcation in 12-18 months."

## Multi-archetype groups (a warning sign)

If multiple firms in one group fit different archetypes, the group is likely splitting. Two outcomes possible:
1. One archetype wins and group consolidates around that posture.
2. Group bifurcates and one becomes two on the strategic-group map at next analysis.

Note the multi-archetype condition in the output; do not paper over it by picking one.

## How archetype links to mobility barriers

Each archetype is protected by specific mobility barriers:
- **Consolidator** — scale-economy gap, supplier-contract lock-in
- **Innovator/specialist** — capability/talent stack, IP, customer intimacy
- **Platform orchestrator** — network effects, ecosystem position (keystone vs participant)
- **Vertically-integrated** — capital base, cornered resource access
- **Asset-light operator** — brand, partner-network coverage

When naming an archetype, name the corresponding barrier in step 6 (mobility barriers).
