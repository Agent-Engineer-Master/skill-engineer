# Mobility Barriers

Mobility barriers (Caves & Porter 1977) are structural forces that prevent firms in one strategic group from credibly repositioning into another group inside the same industry. They are distinct from entry barriers, which protect the industry from outsiders.

## Mobility barriers ≠ entry barriers

| | Entry barrier | Mobility barrier |
|---|---|---|
| Protects | Industry from outsiders | Group from other groups inside industry |
| Origin | Industry-level structure | Group-level positioning |
| Erodes when | Industry deregulates, tech shifts | Group boundaries shift, value chain reconfigures |
| Example | FDA approval for medtech | Premium-brand position requires 10-year brand-build |

An industry can have low entry barriers (easy for outsiders to enter the lowest group) and high mobility barriers (very hard for low-group firms to climb to premium group). This is common in retail, hospitality, and SaaS.

## The 8 mobility-barrier categories

### Classical (Caves/Porter 1977)

1. **Scale economies** — group leaders operate at volumes that drive unit cost 30%+ below sub-scale entrants. Repositioning requires matching scale; capital and time required are prohibitive.
2. **Product differentiation** — group leaders own brand or product positioning that takes years to replicate. New entrant can copy product but not perceived value.
3. **Switching costs** — group's customers face high switching cost (data migration, integration, retraining). A would-be repositioner must overcome that gravitational pull.
4. **Channel access** — group leaders have locked-in channel relationships (exclusive distributors, prime shelf, integrated EHR app stores). Reaching the group's customers without the channel is prohibitive.
5. **Capital requirements** — group operates with capital base (factories, fleet, balance sheet) the would-be repositioner cannot match.

### 2024+ extensions (platform / AI / ecosystem era)

6. **Data-asset accumulation** — group leaders have data flywheel (e.g., usage data, training data, behavioral data) that grows with scale. Repositioner cannot acquire equivalent data without operating at the same scale for the same time. Highly relevant in AI-native industries.
7. **Ecosystem position** — group leader is keystone (sets rules, takes platform rent); other groups are participants. Repositioning participant→keystone is near-impossible once the ecosystem is established. See Iansiti & Levien (Keystone Advantage).
8. **Cornered resource access** — group leaders have exclusive supply contracts, talent clusters, IP, or regulatory licenses. Repositioner faces "you can't get the thing" wall.

## Assessment process

For each pair of **adjacent** strategic groups (groups close to each other on the 2-axis map), name ≥1 mobility barrier that prevents firms in group A from credibly repositioning to group B (or vice versa).

Strong evidence for a barrier:
- **Failed repositioning attempts** — name a firm that tried and failed (this is the gold standard).
- **Long observed stability** — group composition unchanged for 5+ years despite obvious profit incentive to reposition.
- **Asymmetric cost** — quantify the cost or time required to acquire what the other group has.

Weak evidence:
- "It's hard to do." (No mechanism named.)
- "They have brand." (No quantification or comparison.)

## When mobility barriers erode

In Arena and Contested mature markets, mobility barriers weaken. Common erosion mechanisms:

- **Tech shift collapses scale advantage** (e.g., cloud collapsed on-prem scale moats; AI is collapsing service-labor moats).
- **Channel disintermediation** (D2C collapses retail-channel barriers; app stores collapse OS-vendor channels).
- **Data democratization** (open datasets, synthetic data, regulatory data-portability rules collapse data-flywheel barriers).
- **Ecosystem fragmentation** (multi-homing erodes keystone position).

Flag eroding barriers explicitly — they predict group-structure shifts in 2-3 years.

## Mobility-barrier matrix format

In the output, present as a matrix where rows and columns are strategic groups, and cells name the barrier(s) preventing A→B movement. Cells on the diagonal are blank.

| From \\ To | Group A | Group B | Group C |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Group A** | — | scale + brand | data flywheel + ecosystem position |
| **Group B** | switching costs | — | cornered talent |
| **Group C** | channel access | capability stack | — |

For ≥3 adjacent-group pairs (the most-likely repositioning paths), elaborate with one paragraph: mechanism, evidence (with V/C/A/I tag), and direction of travel (eroding / stable / strengthening).
