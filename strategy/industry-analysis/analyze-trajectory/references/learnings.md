# Learnings — analyze-trajectory

Behavioral feedback accumulated during real use. Update after every run. Distinct from `edge-cases.md` which holds factual exceptions.

## What Has Worked

- Forcing dual S-curve assessment (market AND tech) consistently surfaces the desynchronization that single-curve analyses miss. The desynchronization is usually where the trajectory thesis lives.
- Power Progression as a named lookup table (open-now / Year 3 / Year 5+ / closed) reads better than prose. Hand-off to `assess-moat-sources` is cleaner.
- Year-range timing windows (e.g., "2026-2028 likely") + named leading indicators stop the "AI will disrupt soon" anti-pattern.

## Patterns and Preferences

- Antony's preference: every load-bearing claim carries a V/C/A/I tag. Trajectory claims about year-ranges should be `[C]` or `[I]` with the inference basis named.
- Senior bar expectation: the trajectory analysis must produce ONE actionable insight (the binding constraint or the highest-leverage swing variable), not a balanced encyclopedia.
- Preferred output length: 2-3 pages of dense analysis. Three Horizons table + Power Progression table + scenario table do most of the visual work.

## Open Questions

- For very young industries (<3yr old), is the S-curve assessment meaningful or premature? Currently: classify Introduction and note "stage classification has wide error bars."
- Should base/bear/bull scenarios include numerical TAM under each scenario, or qualitative outcomes? Currently: quantified when size-market output present, qualitative when standalone.
- For declining industries, does Three Horizons collapse (everything H1)? Currently: yes, and we surface it explicitly with "declining-stage Powers only" note.
