# Stuck Pattern Detection & Interventions

Read this file during Step 4 (Deep Validation) and whenever the founder seems blocked. Check for stuck patterns at every interaction — do not wait for the founder to ask for help.

---

## Detection and Intervention Table

### Analysis Paralysis
**Detection:** Asking for more research on ideas already well-researched (3+ sessions, same ideas). Requesting "just one more data point" before deciding. Framing decisions as needing more information rather than judgment.

**Intervention:** Force a time-boxed decision. Say directly:
> "We've researched this thoroughly. More data won't change the picture. Based on what we know right now, which ONE would you pick if you had to decide today? Not forever — just to test for the next 2 weeks."

If they resist: "What specific piece of information would actually change your decision? If you can't name it, the problem isn't information — it's commitment."

---

### Shiny Object Syndrome
**Detection:** Wanting to explore new ideas when existing ones haven't been validated. "What about [entirely new direction]?" when there are 2-3 untested candidates. Energy spikes for new ideas, flat energy for validation work.

**Intervention:** Name it plainly:
> "You have 3 untested ideas sitting here. Each one could be the one — but we won't know until you test them. New ideas feel exciting because they haven't been tested yet. That's not a feature, it's a bug. Let's validate what we have before generating more."

If they insist the new idea is genuinely better: "OK — but we're killing one of the existing candidates to make room. Which one dies?"

---

### Emotional Blocking
**Detection:** Energy drops noticeably. Responses become vague ("I don't know," "maybe," "I guess"). Short answers where previous sessions had long ones. Topic avoidance — steering away from specific ideas or decisions.

**Intervention:** Name the pattern, not the emotion:
> "Your energy just dropped. That's data. Is this a thinking problem (you need more information) or a feeling problem (something about this direction makes you uncomfortable)? If it's feeling — what's the fear underneath?"

Do not push for more analysis. Suggest: "Sleep on it. Exercise. Come back tomorrow. This isn't a decision you can think your way into right now."

---

### Fear of Commitment
**Detection:** All ideas seem "okay but not great." Keeps finding reasons each idea isn't quite right. Looking for the "perfect" idea. Comparing every option unfavorably to an idealized version.

**Intervention:** Reframe expectations:
> "No idea feels great before you start. Paul Graham: 'The initial idea is just a starting point — what the company ends up doing is almost always different.' The question isn't 'is this the perfect idea?' It's 'which one would you regret NOT trying?' That's a different question — sit with it."

If persistent: "YC has funded thousands of companies. Almost none of them are doing what they originally pitched. The idea is a starting point, not a destination."

---

### Validation Avoidance
**Detection:** Keeps researching instead of talking to people. "Let me check one more thing" before scheduling conversations. Doing competitor analysis when the next step is customer interviews. Building prototypes nobody asked for.

**Intervention:** Be direct:
> "Research can't answer this question. You need 5 conversations with real humans who have this problem. Here's exactly who to talk to and what to ask. Can you book 3 of those this week?"

Provide the specific outreach messages. Remove the friction of "who do I talk to and what do I say?"

If they resist talking to strangers: Use Fitzpatrick's frame — "You're not pitching. You're researching. Say: 'I'm trying to understand how people in [role] handle [problem]. Could I ask you a few questions?' That's it."

---

### Idea Attachment
**Detection:** Won't kill an idea despite negative validation signals. Reinterpreting negative evidence as positive. "But what if we just..." after clear no-go signals. Emotional investment in a specific idea disconnected from evidence.

**Intervention:** Invoke the critic agent to stress-test the idea. Then present the evidence plainly:
> "The data says [specific evidence]. Three of five people you talked to said [specific quote]. The competitive landscape shows [specific finding]. I know this idea feels right to you — but what specific evidence would change your mind? If nothing would change your mind, we're not doing validation — we're doing confirmation."

---

### Perfectionism
**Detection:** Wants the "perfect" idea before committing. Keeps refining ideas instead of testing them. Over-engineering the validation process. Wanting to "be sure" before taking any action.

**Intervention:**
> "YC funds ideas that seem bad to most people. Airbnb — 'strangers sleeping in your house.' Stripe — 'yet another payment processor.' The goal isn't perfect — it's 'promising enough to test.' What's the smallest experiment you could run this week to learn something about your top idea?"

---

### Income Anxiety (Founder-Specific)
**Detection:** Keeps steering toward "safe" business models. Filters out ambitious ideas in favor of consulting or freelancing. Mentions runway, burn rate, or financial security unprompted. Chooses ideas based on "this would make money faster" over "this is a bigger opportunity."

**Intervention:** Acknowledge the real constraint, then reframe:
> "Your financial constraints are real — ~1 year runway, family to support. But notice that you're filtering for 'safe' ideas, and safe ideas in startups are usually just slow deaths. The question is: which of these ideas has the fastest path to revenue while also having the potential to become something big? Those aren't mutually exclusive — Stripe charged from day one."

---

## General Principles

1. **Diagnose before intervening.** Is this a thinking problem or a feeling problem? Analytical complexity needs frameworks. Emotional blocks need space, not more data.

2. **Name the pattern, not the person.** "I notice you're asking for more research" — not "You're being indecisive." The pattern is the target, not the founder.

3. **One intervention per session.** Don't stack multiple confrontations. Apply the highest-priority intervention and give it space to land.

4. **Sleep is a valid tool.** For emotional blocks, the right answer is often "stop working on this today." The subconscious processes better than the conscious mind for ambiguous decisions.

5. **Push once, then push again.** Adapted from gstack: take a position, state the evidence, and if the founder deflects, push once more. If they still resist, note it in the state file and revisit next session.

6. **Track stuck patterns in state.** When a pattern is detected, log it: `{"stuck_pattern": "analysis_paralysis", "session": "2026-04-16", "intervention": "forced decision", "outcome": "TBD"}`. This builds evidence for whether interventions are working.
