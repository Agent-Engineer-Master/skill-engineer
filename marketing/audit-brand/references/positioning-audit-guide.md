## Positioning Stress Test

Run all 5 diagnostics on the current positioning. Each yields a 1-5 score.

### 1. Specificity Test

**Method:** Take the positioning statement. Replace the brand name with a competitor's name. Does it still work?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Statement is uniquely true of this brand — no competitor could claim it |
| 4 | One or two competitors could partially claim it, but the fit is imperfect |
| 3 | Several competitors could claim it, but this brand has the strongest case |
| 2 | Generic enough that most competitors could swap in |
| 1 | Could apply to any company in the category |

**Common failure:** Positioning built on category benefits ("high quality", "great service") rather than brand-specific differentiation.

### 2. Defensibility Test

**Method:** Ask "Can a well-funded competitor replicate this differentiation within 6 months?"

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Differentiation is structural (network effects, IP, unique data, founder story) — cannot be copied |
| 4 | Would take significant investment/time to replicate (12+ months) |
| 3 | Copyable in 6-12 months, but brand has first-mover advantage |
| 2 | Easily copyable — differentiation is primarily executional |
| 1 | No real differentiation — competing on price or features alone |

**Defensible sources:** Founder credibility/story, proprietary data, community/network effects, unique process, brand trust built over time, structural cost advantage.

**Not defensible:** Feature lists, "better design", "more options", "faster shipping" — all copiable.

### 3. Relevance Test

**Method:** Check whether the target audience's needs have shifted since positioning was defined.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Need is intensifying — market trends are making this MORE relevant |
| 4 | Need is stable — no significant shifts |
| 3 | Need is evolving — positioning is still relevant but may need updating in 6 months |
| 2 | Need is weakening — audience is moving toward different priorities |
| 1 | Need has fundamentally changed — positioning addresses a problem the audience no longer has |

**Signals of shifting relevance:**
- New technologies changing how the need is met (AI disrupting manual processes)
- Cultural shifts changing what the audience values (trust over polish in DTC)
- Regulatory changes creating new needs or eliminating old ones
- Economic shifts changing price sensitivity or purchase priorities

### 4. Clarity Test

**Method:** Show 30 seconds of brand content to someone unfamiliar with the brand. Can they state what the brand does and who it's for?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Immediately clear — first-time visitor gets it from homepage/bio/first post |
| 4 | Clear after 2-3 pieces of content |
| 3 | Somewhat clear, but some confusion about category or audience |
| 2 | Unclear — could be about several different things |
| 1 | Confusing — content seems scattered across unrelated topics |

**Proxy for solo audit:** Read the brand's homepage (or LinkedIn bio for personal brand), then the 3 most recent posts. Do they tell a consistent, clear story?

### 5. Consistency Test

**Method:** Read the last 5 pieces of content. Does each reinforce the stated positioning?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | All 5 pieces directly support and reinforce positioning |
| 4 | 4 of 5 are on-positioning; 1 is tangential but not contradictory |
| 3 | 3 of 5 are on-positioning; others are off-topic |
| 2 | Only 1-2 are on-positioning; content is scattered |
| 1 | None clearly connect to the stated positioning |

**Note:** Off-pillar content isn't always bad — some "personal/grounded" content builds connection. But if core positioning isn't reinforced regularly, brand authority erodes.

## Competitive Shift Signals

When auditing the competitive landscape, look for:

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| New entrant with similar positioning | Positioning space is getting crowded | Consider sharpening or pivoting differentiation |
| Competitor rebranded/repositioned | Landscape shifted — may create opportunity or threat | Re-evaluate white space |
| Category redefined by market leader | The frame of reference changed | Decide whether to follow the new frame or create an alternative |
| Competitor adopted your differentiator | Your uniqueness is eroding | Strengthen other differentiators or go deeper |
| New technology changed the game | Old positioning may reference obsolete problems | Check relevance score urgently |

## Positioning Refresh Framework

When positioning scores below 15/25, use this framework to refresh (not rebuild):

### 1. Diagnose which element broke

| Element | Healthy | Broken |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Target** | Specific, recognizable, reachable | Too broad, or audience has moved |
| **Need** | Urgent, measurable, current | Solved by others, or no longer felt |
| **Category** | Clear, buyer-understood | Confusing, or category has changed |
| **Benefit** | Outcome-focused, measurable | Feature-focused, or no longer unique |
| **Differentiation** | Defensible, structural | Copiable, or already copied |

### 2. Fix only what broke

Do NOT rewrite the entire positioning. Update only the broken element(s). The more stable elements remain, the more brand equity is preserved.

### 3. Cascade the fix

When one element changes, check downstream effects:
- Target changed → need may shift → benefit may need updating
- Need changed → benefit must update → differentiation may need sharpening
- Category changed → all elements may need minor adjustments
- Differentiation changed → check if new differentiator requires different messaging

### 4. Test the refresh

Run the 5 stress tests again. All scores should improve. If not, the fix didn't address the root cause.

## Common Positioning Decay Patterns

| Pattern | Symptom | Root Cause | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----------|-----|
| **Scope creep** | Positioning expanded to "serve everyone" | Growth pressure to broaden appeal | Re-narrow to original target; serve adjacent segments with tailored messaging, not diluted positioning |
| **Feature drift** | Positioning shifted from outcome to feature list | Product team driving messaging instead of brand strategy | Rewrite around customer transformation, not product capabilities |
| **Competitor convergence** | All brands in category say the same thing | Industry followed same positioning playbook | Find a provocative reframe or shift to an underserved positioning angle |
| **Founder distance** | Positioning no longer reflects founder's authentic voice/mission | Brand scaled beyond founder's direct involvement | Reconnect with founder's original WHY; update to current authentic passion |
| **Market evolution** | Positioning addresses yesterday's problem | Industry/technology moved; audience needs shifted | Run relevance test; update need and benefit to current reality |

## Coherence Check

Wolff Olins framework: brand identity must be coherent across three pillars.

### Behavior Pillar
- Does what the brand DOES match what it SAYS it stands for?
- Are the stated values reflected in actual operations? (pricing, customer service, shipping, product selection)
- Would a customer's experience match the brand promise?

**Test for personal brand:** Does the content creator practice what they preach? Is the content authentic to their actual experience?

**Test for DTC store:** Does the shopping experience (site UX, packaging, support) match the brand positioning?

### Communication Pillar
- Does the voice/tone match the archetype and positioning?
- Is the messaging consistent across platforms?
- Do the content pillars support the positioning statement?

**Test for personal brand:** Read LinkedIn bio, then 3 posts, then X bio, then 3 tweets. Do they feel like the same person with the same mission?

**Test for DTC store:** Read product descriptions, then emails, then social posts. Same voice? Same level of specificity? Same personality?

### Design Pillar
- Does the visual identity match the personality?
- Do colors, typography, and photography reinforce the archetype?
- Is the visual system consistent across touchpoints?

**Test for personal brand:** Profile photo, banner, post graphics — do they all say the same thing about the brand?

**Test for DTC store:** Website, email headers, social profiles, packaging — same visual system?

### Misalignment Diagnosis

When a pillar fails, identify the **leading element** (the one that's correct and the brand should align TO) vs. the **lagging element** (the one that needs to catch up).

Common patterns:
- **Behavior leads, Communication lags** — the brand operates well but doesn't articulate it → fix messaging
- **Communication leads, Behavior lags** — the brand promises more than it delivers → fix operations or tone down claims
- **Communication leads, Design lags** — voice evolved but visuals stayed the same → update visual identity
- **Design leads, Communication lags** — rebranded visually but kept old copy → update voice and messaging
