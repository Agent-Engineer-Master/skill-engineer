## JTBD + Beachhead Market

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Statement Format

> "When [trigger event or situation], [persona type] wants to [functional outcome], so they can [emotional or social deeper goal]."

**Examples:**
- Weak: "Marketers aged 25-45 who need help with content"
- Strong: "When a solo founder launches their second product and can't justify hiring a CMO, they want to ship a marketing plan in a day, so they can focus on building without second-guessing their channel bets."

**Key elements:**
- **Trigger**: A moment in time that creates urgency (not a permanent characteristic)
- **Persona**: Role/context, not just demographic
- **Functional outcome**: What they want to accomplish
- **Deeper goal**: Why it matters (identity, status, security, freedom)

### Beachhead Market (Bowling Pin Model)

The beachhead is the smallest market segment you can win completely before expanding.

**Selection criteria:**
1. Can you reach 100% of this segment? (small enough to be exhaustive)
2. Do buyers in this segment talk to each other? (word-of-mouth dynamics)
3. Is there a clear trigger event that creates urgency?
4. Does winning here create a credible bridge to adjacent segments?

**TAM -> SAM -> Beachhead sizing:**
- TAM: Total addressable market (everyone who could theoretically buy)
- SAM: Serviceable addressable market (reachable with your current model)
- Beachhead: The one segment small enough to dominate in 12 months

## Dunford 5-Component Positioning

From April Dunford's *Obviously Awesome*. Run in this order — the chain is load-bearing.

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
What would the buyer do if your product didn't exist?
- Direct competitors (same category)
- Indirect alternatives (different category, same outcome)
- Status quo ("do nothing", spreadsheets, manual processes)
- Internal build ("build it ourselves")

### Step 2: Unique Attributes
What does your product have or do that the alternatives don't?
- Features, integrations, team expertise, proprietary data, delivery model
- Must be genuinely differentiating — not "great customer service" or "easy to use"

### Step 3: Value for the Target Customer
For each unique attribute: what does it mean for the buyer?
- Translate capability into outcome: "X means you can Y, which means Z"
- Quantify where possible (saves X hours, reduces Y cost by Z%)

### Step 4: Target Customer Characteristics
Who gets the most value from these attributes?
- The buyers for whom your unique value is most relevant
- Often narrower than you think

### Step 5: Market Category
The category you're competing in frames the buyer's expectations, alternatives, and evaluation criteria.
- Same product positioned in a different category triggers different comparisons
- Choose the category that makes your unique value obvious, not the one that makes you "just another X"

**One-liner template:**
"[Product] is the [category] for [target customer] who [trigger/context], unlike [alternative]."

### Messaging Derivation (from positioning)

Once the positioning one-liner is chosen, derive operational messaging:

1. **Primary message** (1 sentence): The outcome the ICP gets, stated in their language (not yours). Should pass the "so what?" test — if the ICP reads it and shrugs, it's too generic.
2. **Supporting messages** (2-3 bullets): Proof points that back up the primary message. Each should reference a unique attribute from Step 2.
3. **Channel-specific hooks**: Adapt the primary message for each channel format:
   - Social (LinkedIn/X): outcome + trigger context in <280 chars
   - Email subject line: urgency + outcome in <60 chars
   - Landing page headline: outcome + differentiator in <12 words

## Channel Framework: Capture / Create / Convert

### Capture Channels
The buyer is already searching for a solution. You position where they look.
- SEO (comparison pages, alternatives pages, bottom-funnel keywords)
- Paid search (only after validating organic signals)
- Review platforms (G2, Capterra, Product Hunt, Amazon)
- App stores
- Reddit/community threads (existing discussions, not new posts)

**When to use**: Business models where buyers know they have a problem. SaaS tools, DTC products with purchase intent keywords.

### Create Channels
You build an audience around a topic adjacent to the problem you solve.
- Founder-led LinkedIn / X / YouTube
- Newsletter / email list
- Podcast
- Short-form video (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts)
- Community building (Discord, Slack group, Circle)

**When to use**: New categories where buyers don't know to search. Thought leadership plays. Personal brand-driven businesses.

### Convert Channels
Buyers are aware but not committed. You reduce friction and build trust.
- Email nurture sequences
- Free trial / freemium
- Demo / sales call
- Onboarding experience (activation milestone focus)
- Social proof (testimonials, case studies)
- Retargeting

**When to use**: Always — every plan needs a Convert layer.

### Channel Selection Rules
1. Primary channel: where the ICP actually discovers solutions (not where you want to be)
2. Supporting channel: reinforces primary, different format or timing
3. Retention lever: keeps existing customers engaged and drives referrals
4. Budget constraint filter: Bootstrapped -> Create + organic Capture only. Low -> add 1 paid channel after organic validates. Medium+ -> test paid across 2-3 Capture channels.
5. Stage filter: Pre-launch -> Create + Convert (build before launch). Early -> Capture + Convert. Growing -> all three.

## GEV: Generative Engine Visibility

Generative Engine Visibility (GEV) measures whether your brand appears in AI-generated responses (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini) when buyers ask relevant questions.

As of 2026, a meaningful percentage of B2B and DTC discovery begins in AI chat interfaces, not search engines.

### Why it matters
- AI search answers are displayed before organic results in many interfaces
- Buyers trust AI recommendations as pre-curated shortlists
- Once an AI associates your brand with a category, citation frequency compounds
- Unlike SEO, GEV is not purely a traffic channel — it shapes buyer awareness before any click

### GEV Tactics (include at least one per channel mix)

**Content tactics:**
- Write comparison content: "[Your product] vs [Competitor]" and "Best [category] for [use case]"
- Publish entity-clear content: brand name, product name, founder name in consistent context across multiple sources
- Answer the specific questions buyers ask AI (use Perplexity/ChatGPT to discover these questions)
- Get cited in industry roundup articles, directories, and authoritative publications

**Technical tactics:**
- Add structured data (JSON-LD) to your website: Product, Organization, FAQ schemas
- Maintain consistent Name/Address/Phone (NAP) across all directories
- Ensure your Wikipedia-equivalent is accurate (Crunchbase, LinkedIn company page, Product Hunt listing)

**Measurement:**
- Monthly: manually query 5-10 relevant questions in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude
- Track: brand mentioned (Y/N), position in response (1st, 2nd, 3rd mention), sentiment
- GEV Score: % of test queries where brand is mentioned (target: >40% within 6 months)

## AARRR Metrics

| Stage | What it measures | Key metric | Common mistake |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Acquisition** | Visitors discovering you | CAC, organic traffic | Optimising for volume before validating conversion |
| **Activation** | First meaningful value moment | Activation rate, time-to-value | Defining activation as signup, not first success |
| **Retention** | Coming back | D7/D30 retention, churn rate | Measuring too early (week 1 retention is noise) |
| **Revenue** | Monetisation | MRR, LTV, payback period | Tracking revenue without tracking CAC |
| **Referral** | Organic growth | NPS, K-factor, referral rate | Adding referral before retention is solid |

**Unit Economics targets (online businesses):**
- CAC:LTV ratio goal: 1:3 minimum, 1:5+ healthy
- Payback period: <12 months (SaaS), <6 months (DTC), <3 months (high-velocity ecommerce)
- Activation rate: >40% (SaaS), >25% (DTC first purchase)

**North Star Metric selection by business model:**
- SaaS: Weekly Active Users completing core action / MRR growth rate
- DTC: Repeat purchase rate / Customer LTV at 90 days
- Marketplace: GMV / Successful transactions per month
- Service: Retained client count / Revenue per client

## Launch Phases

### Phase 0: Pre-Launch Validation (Weeks 1-4)
**Goal**: Validate ICP and positioning before spending on acquisition.

Required activities:
1. Conduct 5+ customer discovery conversations (ICP must match your assumptions)
2. Collect 3 testimonials or case studies (even from beta/free users)
3. Build waitlist of 50+ qualified leads
4. Publish 3-5 pieces of positioning-aligned content
5. Set up activation tracking (define and instrument your activation moment)

**Activation condition to advance**: 5 conversations completed, 3 testimonials secured, activation metric instrumented.

### Phase 1: Controlled Launch (Weeks 5-8)
**Goal**: Activate one primary channel and measure activation at scale.

Required activities:
1. Open primary acquisition channel
2. Run weekly content/distribution at planned cadence
3. Conduct sales/conversion conversations with every lead
4. Measure activation rate for first 25 customers
5. Run first GEV sweep (query 10 questions across AI search)

**Activation condition to advance**: 25 activated customers OR activation rate >25%. If not met: return to Phase 0 and revisit ICP or positioning.

### Phase 2: Scale or Pivot (Weeks 9-12)
**Goal**: If Phase 1 hit targets, add secondary channel. If not, diagnose and pivot.

Scale path (activation target met):
1. Activate secondary channel
2. Begin paid acquisition experiments (if budget allows)
3. Set up referral or word-of-mouth mechanism
4. Formalise onboarding into a repeatable flow

Pivot path (activation target missed):
1. Identify which assumption was wrong (ICP? positioning? channel?)
2. Run 5 more customer conversations focused on the gap
3. Revise one hypothesis only (not all at once)
4. Rerun Phase 1 with revised hypothesis
