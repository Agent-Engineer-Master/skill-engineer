# Marketing Plan Eval Rubric

Derived from analysis of real GTM strategy documents (HubSpot, Cognism, Common Thread Collective).
Use this rubric to score marketing plan outputs on 8 discriminating dimensions.

**Score scale**: 1 = weak/generic, 2 = adequate/partial, 3 = strong/specific

**Maximum score**: 24 points
**Benchmarks**: AI-generated generic plan: 9-13. Mediocre human plan: 12-16. Strong plan: 20-24.

---

## Dimension 1: ICP Specificity

Whether the ideal customer profile goes beyond demographics to capture the trigger, context, and outcome that drives purchase.

- **Score 1**: Defined by demographics or firmographics only. No trigger moment or JTBD. Could describe millions of non-buyers. Example: "marketers aged 25-45 who need help with content."
- **Score 2**: Includes a pain point or role context. Identifies who and roughly why — but trigger moment, urgency, and desired outcome are absent or vague.
- **Score 3**: Includes a named JTBD with trigger moment and desired outcome. Could identify a specific individual and predict when they become a buyer. Example: "When a solo founder launches their second product and can't justify hiring a CMO, they want to ship a marketing plan in a day, so they can focus on building without second-guessing channel bets."

---

## Dimension 2: Competitive Landscape Quality

Whether the plan maps the competitive terrain with enough specificity to inform positioning and channel decisions.

- **Score 1**: No competitive analysis, or competitors mentioned only by name without positioning claims, vulnerabilities, or white space identification. "There are several competitors in this space."
- **Score 2**: Names specific competitors with their positioning claims or pricing, but does not identify where they cluster, where open ground exists, or which competitor is the displacement target.
- **Score 3**: Maps 3-5 competitors with positioning claims, primary channels, pricing, and vulnerabilities. Identifies where competitors cluster, names the open ground, and specifies the displacement target. White space directly feeds into the positioning choice.

---

## Dimension 3: Positioning Distinctiveness

Whether the plan names real competitive alternatives and explains a specific mechanism for winning.

- **Score 1**: Feature list or generic value proposition ("fast, easy, affordable"). No named alternatives. No articulation of why this product, for this buyer, over anything else.
- **Score 2**: Acknowledges a competitive category, claims differentiation, but the mechanism is generic or unsubstantiated. Names a competitor type but not the specific axis of differentiation.
- **Score 3**: Names actual alternatives the buyer would evaluate (including "do nothing" and spreadsheets). Identifies unique attributes competitors lack. Translates attributes into differentiated value for the ICP. Follows Dunford's chain: alternatives -> unique attributes -> value -> target buyer. Includes operational messaging (primary message, supporting messages, channel hooks).

---

## Dimension 4: Channel Selection Rationale

Whether channel choices are derived from ICP behaviour and business stage, not from generic popularity.

- **Score 1**: Lists channels without explaining why each reaches the ICP. Mix could have been copied from any generic plan. No stage-appropriateness or prioritisation logic.
- **Score 2**: Explains the role of each channel at a high level. Acknowledges audience fit but lacks specificity about how the ICP actually discovers solutions, or why this mix suits this stage.
- **Score 3**: Channel selection is derived from a buyer journey map showing how the ICP discovers and evaluates solutions at each stage. Explains why this channel, for this buyer, at this business stage. Includes channel sequencing and prioritisation criteria (e.g. organic before paid, once CAC is validated). Budget allocation is connected to channel choice.

---

## Dimension 5: Launch Sequencing Logic

Whether the plan has a phased, milestone-based rollout rather than a single launch event.

- **Score 1**: Launch is a single event or date. No phasing, no sequencing of activities, no dependencies. Reads as "we will launch on [date] and do marketing."
- **Score 2**: Includes pre/launch/post phases, but milestones are vague or arbitrary. No stated logic for why things happen in this order or what must be true before advancing.
- **Score 3**: Each phase has explicit activation conditions (e.g. "Phase 1 ends when we have 25 activated customers — only then do we open secondary channel"). Dependencies are named. Early phases derisk later phases. Includes consideration of what breaks the plan.

---

## Dimension 6: Pricing and Monetisation Logic

Whether pricing is derived from value delivered and buyer willingness-to-pay, not from cost or competitor copying.

*Note: Mark N/A for pre-revenue ideas where pricing has not yet been set. Penalise plans that invent a number without rationale.*

- **Score 1**: Pricing stated without justification, or justified only by competitor benchmarking ("20% cheaper than X"). No connection to value delivered, buyer segment, or model rationale.
- **Score 2**: Pricing model explained with some market positioning rationale. References competitor pricing. Does not connect price to the value outcome the ICP receives or to willingness-to-pay evidence.
- **Score 3**: Pricing derived from buyer value and validated willingness-to-pay. Model type (flat, usage, freemium, tiered) justified by GTM motion and ICP buying behaviour. Includes logic for how pricing evolves with scale (raise triggers tied to measurable conditions, not volume).

---

## Dimension 7: Success Metrics and Unit Economics

Whether the plan sets measurable targets grounded in business economics, not vanity metrics.

- **Score 1**: Success defined by vanity metrics only (followers, impressions, traffic, "brand awareness"). No CAC, LTV, payback, or revenue targets. Cannot determine whether GTM is working.
- **Score 2**: Includes revenue or customer targets with a timeline. May reference CAC or conversion rates. Metrics are measurable but relationships between them are not stated (no CAC:LTV logic, no payback threshold).
- **Score 3**: Metrics form a coherent unit economics model: states CAC target, LTV assumption, payback period threshold, and what each implies for channel investment. Metrics are time-phased and tied to launch phases. Includes measurement chain (Budget -> CPM -> CTR -> CPC -> Conv -> CAC -> LTV:CAC) and pre-launch analytics setup. States what metric, at what threshold, would cause the plan to pivot or stop.

---

## Dimension 8: Coherence Across the Plan

Whether ICP, competitive landscape, positioning, channels, budget, launch, pricing, and metrics form a logically consistent whole rather than sections written in isolation.

- **Score 1**: Major internal contradictions present (e.g. enterprise ICP but purely self-serve PLG motion; channels that don't reach the stated ICP; success metrics that don't reflect the business model; budget allocation that doesn't match chosen channels).
- **Score 2**: No obvious contradictions but connections between sections are implicit. The reader must infer how ICP drives channel choice. Plan is consistent but not tightly reasoned.
- **Score 3**: Each section explicitly builds on the previous. Competitive landscape feeds positioning. Positioning drives channel selection. Journey map justifies the channel mix. Budget allocation matches the chosen channels. Launch phases connect to KPI targets. When any assumption changes, downstream implications are traceable. Reads as one integrated argument.

---

## Calibration Notes

- A typical AI-generated generic plan scores: D1=1, D2=1, D3=1, D4=1, D5=1, D6=1, D7=2, D8=2 -> **Total: ~10/24**
- A mediocre human business school plan scores: D1=2, D2=1, D3=2, D4=2, D5=1, D6=2, D7=2, D8=1 -> **Total: ~13/24**
- A strong practitioner plan scores: D1=3, D2=3, D3=3, D4=3, D5=3, D6=2, D7=3, D8=3 -> **Total: ~23/24**
- On Dimension 6: competitor benchmarking alone is Score 2, not Score 3. Score 3 requires explicit connection between price point and value outcome for the ICP.
- On Dimension 2: mentioning competitors is Score 1. Mapping their positioning, vulnerabilities, and the resulting white space is Score 3.
